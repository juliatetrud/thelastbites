# Chapter 2 Outline — A Cat, a Pan, a Promise

The first chapter of the rising action. Pip's first solo port. Establishes the chapter rhythm (wordless traversal → monster cleverness puzzle → chef encounter), introduces Pätu, plants the foreshadow that *not all ghosts are good*. The chapter rhythm has flex here — the monster encounter is a frame structure rather than a single beat.

This document is the **narrative outline** for the chapter — its emotional shape, structural beats, and the working assumptions for monster/chef/recipe/clue. Implementation-grade detail (dialogue, cinematic specs, beat-by-beat) will be written separately when this chapter is being built.

**Working title:** *A Cat, a Pan, a Promise.* Provisional. May change.

---

## Position in the arc

Chapter 2 is the **first chapter of the rising action** in the game's seven-beat arc. It is also the *first chapter following the locked chapter rhythm* (wordless traversal → monster encounter → chef encounter). It teaches the player how a chapter shapes itself — and demonstrates that the rhythm has flex in it. The monster encounter does not have to be one tight beat; in Ch2 it's a frame structure that opens the chapter (Haldjas wariness), stretches across the middle (cooperative pan-quest), and resolves *after* the chef encounter as a closing spell-puzzle the Haldjas casts inside the cottage. The principle is preserved — cleverness, not combat — but the staging varies.

It is deliberately gentler than the chapters that follow — its monster is sympathetic, its chef encounter is cooperative cooking rather than testing, its emotional weight is reunion rather than challenge.

---

## Emotional arc

Pip's first solo chapter. Henrik's blessing in his pocket and an empty journal in his hand. The lesson he carries from Ch1: *food carries memory*. The lesson he learns here: *he is the kind of ghost who can do good*. He proves it cooperatively — by helping search for a lost thing, restoring it to its owner, and making a promise to a cat that a guardian spirit has to vouch for.

**What Pip carries in:** The taste-memory ability. The journal. The just-spoken question of why Henrik could see him. Five new ghost-abilities in his hands. A grief he's still figuring out how to hold.

**What Pip learns:** Not all ghosts are good. Some spirits can sense the difference. He himself is a ghost made of bonds — of being fed, being loved, being remembered. He can sense the hands that shaped an object (a new ability). Cats choose where they belong; people don't always get to. A promise has to be sincere, not just spoken. *And* — quietly, through Leida's taste-memory — that an act of welcome can shape a whole life.

**What Pip carries out:** A new ability (object-memory). A companion (Pätu, on her terms). A promise to protect her *as long as she chooses to come aboard*, vouched for by a guardian spirit. The recipe for kodused kotletid. The taste-memory of a young Leida sharing her lunch with a starving boy. A foreshadow he doesn't fully understand yet — that the bad ghosts are out there, and he'll meet them.

## Setting

Two settings, divided by the gangway:

**On the ship (lower decks).** The Mnemosyne's lower passages — narrow, brass-fixtured, deep crimson carpet, sea-blue light through portholes. The wordless traversal happens here. Echo-mice scurry along the baseboards. Pätu is somewhere ahead, a flash of fur in the dim. The Haldjas is with her, sparkling in the corner.

**In Estonia.** A small foggy coastal village. Wooden buildings with sloped roofs. The smell of fish and birch smoke. Leida's cottage sits a little apart from the village — wooden, low-eaved, with a window full of geraniums. Inside: a wood stove, a long pine table, a kettle, dried herbs hanging from the rafters. Outside, near the cottage path: a small altar — a flat stone, a wooden bowl, a faded ribbon — clearly used for years.

**Specific port:** TBD. Real Estonian fishing villages worth considering: Käsmu, Altja, the Lahemaa coast. Smaller is better — village texture, not city.

## The wordless traversal

Echo-mice along the baseboards. Pip moves toward the gangway. As he travels, he glimpses small lost things — an old photograph stuck behind a pipe, a child's mitten dropped between deck plates, a single button. He cannot retrieve them. The traversal's emotional bed is *the ship is full of small lost things, and one of them today is going to be returned*.

Pätu is briefly visible deeper in the ship — surrounded by something Pip can't quite see (the Haldjas's shimmer). The traversal ends as Pip reaches her corner. The monster encounter begins.

**Length:** Brief. 60–90 seconds.

**Hazards:** Echo-mice. Bumping one drains a small amount of strength (instant, sharp). Floating over them avoids it.

**No dialogue. No UI clutter. Just movement, atmosphere, and the sounds of the ship.**

---

## Monster encounter — the Haldjas (opening: wariness)

A protective Estonian house-spirit. Bound to Leida's family for generations. As a favor to Leida, the Haldjas keeps an eye on Pätu — the stray Leida nursed back from cold as a kitten, who grew up free and now lives on the ship. The Haldjas is not Pätu's spirit. She is Leida's, doing kindness work outside her remit.

The Haldjas is sparkling beside Pätu when Pip arrives. She challenges him directly:

> *"Not all of you are good. How do I know which one you are?"*

This is the chapter's first beat of *frightening encounter* — the chill of being looked at by a spirit who is deciding what kind of ghost you are. But it does not resolve here. The Haldjas does not pose a single puzzle and step aside. She names a *task* — Leida has lost her old cast-iron pan, and Pätu has been refusing to leave the ship without it. *Help us find it. We will see what kind of ghost you are.*

Pip is invited to help, on probation.

---

## The cooperative pan-search

The middle of the chapter. Pip, Pätu, and the Haldjas search the lower deck together for Leida's pan.

This is where Pätu's gameplay function is *introduced through gameplay*. She is a soft hint-giver. If Pip is taking too long, she meows by an important spot. If he's heading the wrong way, she sits down and looks at him. The player learns: *the cat is part of how you play.*

Pip can use object-memory on a few wrong objects along the way — a lamp, a brass hook, a coiled rope. Each touch shows him a small unrelated memory (*provisional, can be cut for scope*) — flavor, lore drips, ship history. The point is to teach the player the verb: *touch, focus, see*.

Eventually Pätu meows insistently at a corner. Behind a pipe: a heavy old cast-iron pan, dusty and forgotten.

Pip touches it.

**Object-memory triggers — a new ability, earned here.** Like taste-memory but through an object's history. Pip sees a vision: an old woman cooking meatballs in this pan, feeding a young cat. The old woman is laughing. The cat is Pätu. The vision tells Pip where to go.

**Object-memory differs from taste-memory in that it requires *intent*.** Pip touches and focuses; he can choose not to see. (Taste-memory is involuntary — eat and see.) This is a small but meaningful difference. Object-memory is the *grown-up* version of taste-memory. Pip is learning to direct his gift, not just receive it.

This is the first ability Pip earns outside Chapter 1. It foreshadows the later "possess small objects" ability — object-memory is its passive precursor.

The Haldjas, watching, softens slightly. *"Yes. You are made of bonds. Go on, then — bring her her pan."*

Pip and Pätu cross the gangway. The Haldjas comes with them — she will not enter the village proper, but she travels alongside, sparkling at the periphery, until they reach Leida.

---

## Chef encounter — Leida

Leida is an Estonian grandmother, real and human, living alone in a wooden cottage on the village's edge. Long widowed (provisional). Younger than Henrik but old. She has known the Haldjas her whole life — and her mother knew the Haldjas, and her grandmother before that. The relationship is generational. She fed Pätu as a kitten, nursed her when she was sick, and watches the cat come and go now without resentment. *"She is a free animal. She comes when she wants. I leave the door cracked."*

When Pip arrives, carrying her old cast-iron pan, with her cat trotting ahead of him — Leida's face does something quiet. *"Oh. Oh, you brought her back. And you brought him."* She doesn't ask how. She is the kind of person who takes blessings without questioning them.

The chef puzzle is **cooperative cooking** — Format C (ingredient logic) but tender, not testing. Leida tells Pip the recipe as she works. Pip helps her: passing ingredients in the right order, folding the meat correctly, knowing when the pan is hot enough. Wrong choices are gentle — *"Not yet, Pip. Watch the surface."* The puzzle isn't a test of understanding. It is the *act* of cooking together.

**The recipe earned: kodused kotletid.** Estonian home-style meatballs. Pan-fried in butter. Served with potatoes and sour cream. Unlocked on the recipe website at the chapter's end.

The meal is shared at Leida's pine table. Pätu sits at her feet. The Haldjas sparkles in the doorway, watching, content.

---

## The taste-memory — Leida's lunchbox

After the meal, when Pip takes his first proper bite — taste-memory triggers.

The cottage falls away. Pip is in a school-age girl's body, sitting on a low wall outside a wooden Estonian schoolhouse, in a winter coat. A tin lunch-pail open on her lap. Inside: two *kotletid* her mother packed that morning, still warm-ish from the cloth.

Across the yard, a thin boy is sitting alone in his too-light jacket. No lunch. The kind of hungry that has stopped expecting anything. The girl looks at him for a moment. Then she stands, walks over, sits down beside him on the cold step, and holds out one of her meatballs.

> *(young Leida, in Estonian, voice not yet old:)* *"My mother always packs two. Take it."*

The boy takes it carefully, as if it might break. He eats it slowly, the way someone eats who has learned not to rush food. He doesn't speak. After a while, he hands the pail back to her, empty-handed-thank-you, and they sit on the step with the wind moving in the birches above them. No words. Just the warmth of a meatball passed between cold hands.

The memory shimmers briefly into a second image — the same boy, twenty years older, at Leida's pine table in the cottage we just left, eating the same meatballs, laughing about something. *His face is the face of a husband.* The image lasts a beat and is gone.

Pip is back at the table. Leida is watching him, not surprised. She knows what tasting can do.

> LEIDA: *(quietly)* *He liked them too.*

She does not explain further. The husband's chair is empty. It has been empty a long time. *(Provisional: the chair detail can be added or cut depending on whether the player has noticed the cottage's solitude. The line lands either way.)*

This is the chapter's **meal moment** for the climax pair (replacing the altar offering, which becomes the structural circle-closing beat instead — see Memory pair section below).

It also seeds the recipe-earned beat: the entry that gets written into Pip's journal includes both the recipe and a memory of *kindness shared with a stranger across a cold step*. This memory becomes specifically load-bearing in **Chapter 6's Pocong puzzle** — the second riddle keys to it. *(Cross-reference: `ch06-indonesia-outline.md`. See doc 06's Decisions Log.)*

## The closing puzzle — the Haldjas's spell (sincerity test)

After the meal, as Pip prepares to leave, Pätu makes a small ceremony of it. She walks to the door, turns, looks at Pip, and meows. The narration may surface: *Pätu is asking you something.*

Pip kneels beside her.

> PIP: *I'll protect you, Pätu. As long as you choose to come aboard.*

The light in the cottage dims. The Haldjas's sparkles thicken. Three doors materialize in the room — wood-grain doors that don't belong to any specific architecture. Folk-tale doors. The cottage and Leida and Pätu fade slightly. The Haldjas does not speak. She has cast a spell, and she watches from inside it.

This is the chapter's **cleverness puzzle and sincerity test in one**. Three doors, three offers, one right choice. The right choice is the one where Pip gives without getting. The wrong choices reveal as bad ghosts wearing tempting faces.

The puzzle's central lesson — *give without getting* — is the bible's core verb in three clicks. It teaches the player the rule Pip will need at Greenpoint, where the shadow will offer him a version of the same bargain. Door 3 in particular seeds the climax directly: it offers Pip a glimpse of his grandparents in exchange for a betrayal. He refuses it here. He will refuse it there.

The full design — door content, dialogue, reveals, resolution sequence — lives in `chapter-specs/ch02-sincerity-puzzle-spec.md`. It is the canonical spec; this outline section is a summary.

**The cat-reveal at door 2 is what links the spell to the promise.** When Pip feeds the starving old woman, she unfolds into Pätu. The figure he fed *was* the cat all along. By feeding her without recompense, in a form that didn't reward him for recognizing the bond, Pip has demonstrated exactly the kind of bond-keeping the Haldjas needed to verify. Sincerity verified by the same act that proves moral character.

The Haldjas's resolution line — drafted as *"You saw her. The spell is done. Go."* — is brief. The cottage returns to itself. Pätu walks to the door.

---

## The altar — Leida's offering

As Pip and Pätu walk down the cottage path, Leida steps outside with a small wrapped meatball. She does not give it to Pip. She walks past him, to the small altar near the path — a flat stone, a wooden bowl, a faded ribbon — and places the meatball in the bowl.

It is clearly something she has done many times before. The altar is not new. The bowl has held many offerings.

She speaks softly, not quite to anyone:

> LEIDA: *Thank you, old friend. For watching over her. For watching over them.*

The Haldjas's sparkles thicken at the altar. She accepts the offering. She does not follow Pip and Pätu to the gangway. Her work is done. She stays with Leida.

This is the chapter's structurally important inversion: the circle closes *at the port*, behind Pip, rather than on the ship around him. He and Pätu walk away from a circle they helped complete. They have left something good behind to come aboard.

---

## The return

Pip and Pätu cross the gangway alone. The chapter ends in the lower deck — the corner where the Haldjas had been is now just a corner. Pätu stretches. Pip stands a moment longer.

The chapter's last beat is quiet: a boy and a cat, on a ship, with a promise newly made. Outside, the sea. Somewhere ahead, the next port.

This ending is structurally important because it inverts the usual chapter shape. Most chapters from Ch3 onward will probably end at the port or in a transition cinematic back to the ship. Chapter 2 ends *on the ship*, with the circle closing behind them. The chain of feeding — *the cure for grief is to cook for the next person* — gets modeled, in miniature, at Leida's altar.

## Memory pair for the climax

- **Monster moment (frightening):** The Haldjas's initial wariness. The chill of being looked at by a spirit who is deciding whether you are *good*. The line: *"Not all of you are good."* Pip carries this forward — he will be looked at this way again.
- **Meal moment (warm):** The taste-memory of young Leida and the cold step. *A meatball passed between cold hands.* The first time Pip witnesses, from the inside, *welcome of a stranger* — which becomes one of the chapter's load-bearing memories for Ch6's Pocong puzzle and (provisionally) for the climax.

The altar offering remains a structurally important closing beat — the chain of feeding made visible — but the *carried memory* is now the lunchbox scene. A taste-memory is more transferrable than an observed altar offering, and the lunchbox memory has the precise emotional shape (*welcoming the unwelcomed*) that the back half of the game will pull on.

(This decision is provisional — it can be revisited when the climax is being planned. Either memory works in principle; the lunchbox scene is the stronger pull because it puts Pip *inside* an act of welcome rather than beside one.)

## Abilities earned

- **Object-memory.** Touch a meaningful object, focus, see the hands that used it. New verb, earned at the pan moment. Persists for the rest of the game.

No other new abilities. Pip uses his existing Ch1 abilities (walk through wood, talk through speakers, flicker electricity, float, taste-memory) as needed in the chapter — including, now, the taste-memory at the table.

## What this chapter teaches the player (mechanical literacy)

- **The chapter rhythm.** Wordless traversal → monster encounter → chef encounter — and the rhythm has flex. The monster encounter is a frame, not a beat.
- **The wordless traversal mode.** No dialogue. Movement and float. Echo-creatures as gentle hazards. The strength meter as environmental.
- **The cooperative quest layer.** Some monster encounters are not solved by puzzle alone — they're solved by *helping*. Pip will see this again in later chapters.
- **The chef encounter.** Cleverness puzzle, but warmer. Cooperative cooking. Recipe earned at the end.
- **Taste-memory carries forward.** The Ch1 ability returns — a taste of a chef's recipe shows the chef's own past. The player learns the verb is going to recur. Each chapter's taste-memory becomes a journal entry the player can later draw on.
- **The closing puzzle.** Cleverness puzzle in the monster's idiom — the Haldjas's three-doors spell. Wrong choices gentle, hint-and-retry, no fail state. The form is themed; future chapters' puzzles will look different.
- **The journal grows.** First Ch2-onward entry: recipe component (kodused kotletid → strength), memory component (Leida's lunchbox taste-memory → empathy).
- **A new ability is earned mid-chapter.** Object-memory. Demonstrated immediately at the pan, used again in later chapters.
- **Pätu as a gameplay collaborator.** Soft hint-giver introduced through the pan-search. The player learns to read her.

## Foreshadowing planted

- **Not all ghosts are good.** The Haldjas's warning. This is the seed for the shadow at the climax — Pip is not the first ghost to wash up here, and the bad ones leave a scent.
- **Pätu as bad-ghost detector.** Her gameplay function includes hissing at bad ghosts. Where this lands in later chapters is open.
- **Henrik has fed ghost-children before.** Implied by the Haldjas's ability to immediately classify ghosts. Pip is one of several.
- **Pätu as recurring companion.** Her promise-pact with Pip. Her freedom to come and go. By late game she may follow him more often. She may have a small role at the climax (TBD).
- **Welcome of strangers as a load-bearing verb.** Leida's lunchbox memory plants the gesture. Ch6's Pocong puzzle will pull on it directly. The climax may pull on it again.

- Whether the chapter has any inspectable side-objects in Leida's cottage that lightly drip lore (a violin in the corner that *might* be the same kind the Nøkken plays — a small unspoken connection between the chapters' folklore). Worth deciding when the chapter is being built.
- The exact wording of door 3's offer (the healthy man's "I can take you to see them"). It needs to *ache*. See the spec doc's open questions for the working draft.
- Whether Pätu has any dialogue. She's a cat. She probably doesn't speak. But she might *think* in narration that the player can read — *"Pätu is staring at the corner. She wants you to see something."* — making her a quiet narrator-collaborator. Pull: yes, lightly.
- The wrong-object object-memory flavor visions during the pan-search. Worth a small decision: do they reveal anything (lore drips), or are they purely flavor? Pull: lore drips, quiet ones — small ship-history glimpses that won't pay off until later chapters.
- The full draft of young Leida's voice in the lunchbox memory. The Estonian line is a placeholder. To be developed when the chapter is built. Should be brief — the warmth is in the gesture, not the words.
- Whether the second-image flash (the husband twenty years later at the cottage table) lands as a visible beat or as a felt undertone. Pull: visible but very brief — one or two seconds. The player should *understand* without the memory becoming maudlin.

## What this chapter is NOT

- Not a test of skill. The puzzles are gentle. The traversal is brief.
- Not a tragedy. The chapter ends warmly, with a circle closed behind them.
- Not a place where bad ghosts actually appear. The foreshadow is verbal only. The bad ghosts wait.
- Not the chapter where Pätu becomes Pip's constant companion. She makes a promise *to come and go as she pleases*. Her warming arc is across many chapters, not this one.
- Not a chapter where the Haldjas joins the ship. She stays with Leida. She is Leida's spirit, not the ship's. She does not recur.
- Not Leida's grief story. The taste-memory shows the warm beginning, not the loss. Her widowhood is *implied* by the empty chair and her quiet *"He liked them too,"* and the player does the work. The chapter's emotional center remains Pätu and the promise; the lunchbox scene is texture and seed.
