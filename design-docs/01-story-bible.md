# Story Bible

The narrative foundation for The Last Bites. Mechanics serve this. Art serves this. When in doubt, this is the source of truth.

---

## Premise

Pip is a ghost. He died young, before he got to taste the world.

The afterlife isn't quite what he expected. He can't move on — not yet. He's tethered to the cruise ship he died on, **The Mnemosyne** *(Nem-OSS-uh-nee)*, a vessel that travels the world endlessly, port to port. But there's a way through: he must understand food. Not just eat it, but learn its stories, the people who make it, the regions it comes from. Each meal he completes earns him passage forward.

When he's finally tasted enough — really tasted, really *understood* — the door to the other side will open.

## The Protagonist: Pip

**Full name:** Filip. His grandmother calls him *Filipek* when she's being tender, *Filip* when she's worried. He shortened it to "Pip" himself somewhere around age six — a small American assertion he's quietly proud of.

**Age:** 10-12 years old when he died.

**Background:** Pip's parents died when he was 3-4 years old. The pierogi dinner with all of them — his mom, his dad, Babcia and Dziadek — is his earliest memory, the one at the bottom of the well, the one he was almost too young to keep. After his parents passed, he was raised by his Polish immigrant grandparents in Greenpoint, Brooklyn. They were not wealthy, but they were rich in food traditions — pierogi at Christmas, golabki on Sunday afternoons, the smell of rye and dill always in the apartment. His grandparents' love language was food, and Pip absorbed it.

Every week, his grandparents took him to a different restaurant — sometimes Polish (Karczma, Christina's), sometimes way outside their world (a ramen shop in Manhattan, a Senegalese place in Bed-Stuy). They saved up for these outings. Pip kept a little notebook of every dish he tried and what he thought of it. He also had friends at school whose families fed him things Babcia and Dziadek didn't cook — Jamaican food at one friend's house in particular stayed with him. By age 11, he had reviewed 142 dishes.

**Cause of death:** A heart condition, undiagnosed. He died in his sleep in Cabin 646 of the cruise ship, on the first night of what was supposed to be the trip of a lifetime — his grandparents had saved for years to take him on a global voyage.

**Personality:** Curious, gentle, observant. A foodie's foodie. He's not afraid — he's *interested*. The strangeness of being dead is, to him, a new kind of restaurant menu. He misses his grandparents desperately, but he's a kid, and kids can hold grief and wonder at the same time.

**Visual (present-day ghost):** Small translucent ghost. A simple white form with three soft waves at the bottom. Two small dark eye-dots, a faint pink blush, a tiny dark mouth — that's it. No hair. No clothing. The human details he had in life are not part of his ghost form. He floats just above the ground. When he's happy, his outline glows a little warmer. When he's frightened or sad, his form blurs at the edges.

**Visual (in memory):** The boy he was, before. Short dark brown hair. The oversized chef's apron his grandmother gave him for the trip, the one he was going to learn to cook in. Same eye-dots, same blush, same gentle expression — but with a small human body underneath, with arms and legs and clothing. The apron is beige with a few flour smudges. This Pip appears *only* in flashback memory beats (his own memories, or memories he is given). He does not appear in the present moment of the game.

**Why the form is so spare.** Pip's ghost-form has lost the trappings of the boy he was. The apron meant *I am going to learn to cook* — a small held intention. The hair, the body, the clothing — all the soft particulars of being a person. The ghost has none of that. What remains is essence: eyes, a hint of feeling in the cheeks, a small mouth, a shape. The fullness of him is held inside, waiting to be remembered. The visual difference between ghost-Pip and memory-Pip is a quiet thematic spine of the whole game. Don't break it.

**Powers (revealed gradually across chapters):**
- Walking through walls (Chapter 1)
- Manipulating electricity / making lights flicker (Chapter 1)
- Talking through radios and intercoms (Chapter 1)
- **Floating** — rising from the ground briefly with effort, roughly 1.5 character-heights. Not a jump; he's a ghost, he doesn't push off (Chapter 1, Beat 8 — discovered alongside the electricity ability)
- Tasting food and seeing the cook's memory of making it (Chapter 1, the central mechanic) — involuntary; eat and see
- **Object-memory** — touching a meaningful object and seeing the hands that used it (Chapter 2, earned at Leida's lost cast-iron pan). Requires *intent* — Pip touches and focuses; he can choose not to see. The grown-up version of taste-memory. Foreshadows the later possession ability.
- Possessing small objects briefly (later chapter)
- **Memory-gifting** — manifesting memories Pip has collected and giving them to another being (Chapter 5, earned from Iris). Empathy strengthens this power. It is the most important ability Pip will gain. The journal of recipes and memories Pip collects is the source he draws from. Earned in two beats with Iris (receive a memory through her father's switchblade, then give it back to her in full clarity), then immediately weaponized in the same chapter against the Mamlambo. Input: journal opens, left/right arrows navigate entries, up arrow selects and sends. Visual signature: the same wavy-bloom transition as taste-memory and object-memory, played in reverse — going outward from Pip rather than inward.
- Being seen by people who have themselves lost someone (revealed slowly)

## The Mentor: Henrik

**Role:** The ship's head chef. Norwegian, in his sixties. Has worked on cruise ships for thirty years. Lost his son Erik and his wife Henrietta in a boating accident decades ago — they drowned, he was there and could not reach them — which is why he can see Pip.

**Personality:** Quiet, dry, kind in a gruff way. Not effusive. Doesn't waste words. Treats Pip like a colleague, not a child — which is exactly what Pip needs.

**Function in the story:** The hub character. He stays on the ship across all chapters. When Pip docks at a new port, Henrik gives him a thread to pull — a name, a dish, a rumor. When Pip returns from a port, Henrik is there to talk through what happened. He's also the one who, in the final chapter, finds Pip in the kitchen after the shadow encounter and sees Pip across.

**Backstory:** Henrik's son **Erik** died at age 12, in a boating accident that also killed Henrik's wife, **Henrietta**. They drowned together. Henrik was there. He could not reach them in time. He believes a violin played as he tried — the **Nøkken**, the Norse shape-shifting water-spirit who lures people to drown. He has never said so out loud. He went to sea afterwards, not to escape the water but to be *closer to their spirits* — he believes the dead live in the sea. Standing still in a house with two empty chairs was unbearable; commuting daily toward what took them is the shape his grief settled into. He has been on cruise ships ever since.

In Chapter 1 he tells Pip the Nøkken story as a folktale, in a "you should know what's out there" register. He doesn't say it's about Henrietta. The player should feel it anyway. Chapter 4 surfaces Henrietta as a person Pip knows a little about — name spoken aloud, a few textures (pomegranate molasses, who she was) — but Erik remains unnamed. The full reveal (Erik named, the photograph seen, Henrik's quarters entered) is reserved for the photograph-reveal chapter (Ch6 or Ch7, TBD).

**Why he can see Pip:** Implied in Chapter 1 (Pip wonders aloud how Henrik can see him, after two strangers on the ship walk past Pip without noticing). Confirmed in the photograph-reveal chapter (Ch6 or Ch7, TBD). The reveal: Pip first catches Henrik tucking a photograph away in Chapter 4 but chooses not to ask. Later, Pip earns access to Henrik's quarters by completing a task. The photograph is waiting there — *Erik* — along with a recipe for lefse and gravlaks: Erik's favorite meal, the same meal Henrik served Pip on their first night together. Henrik has been feeding the ghost-child the food he could no longer feed his own son.

**Erik's annual visit:** Erik moved on long ago. He returns once a year, on his birthday, because love reaches across. Erik's birthday falls during one of the rising-action chapters (Ch6 or Ch7, TBD). Henrik becomes withdrawn and sad. Pip notices something is wrong, investigates, and witnesses the reunion. This tells Pip something the bible needs him to know: *moving on is not forgetting. The dead can still come home.* It is the lesson Pip himself will eventually need.

**Visual:** Older Norwegian man, thick gray beard, weathered face. Always has flour on his apron. Smokes a pipe out on the deck at night. Pixel sprite is taller than Pip but still small in the world.

## The Midpoint Friend: Iris

**Role:** A child ghost from the wreck of *The Lethe*, an Edwardian ocean liner that sank around 1905-1912. Pip meets her in Chapter 5. She is the midpoint character whose resolution rehearses Pip's own.

**Background:** Iris died in the wreck while eating dinner with her family. Her meal — including the brussels sprouts she didn't want to finish — was unfinished when she died. She has been tethered to the wreck for over a century, refusing to finish her meal because finishing means forgetting her family.

**How they meet:** *The Mnemosyne* stalls overnight directly above the wreck of *The Lethe*. The proximity lets Iris reach up. She squeezes through the floorboards of one of the ship's lower rooms — a *squeeze-and-pop* sound effect — and emerges in front of Pip, both of them shocked to see each other. She is wet from the sea, in Edwardian clothes. While Pip turns to find her a towel, she shakes off all the water like a dog. When he turns back, she is dry. Pip doesn't realize she's a ghost — she becomes translucent only when sad or stubborn. The moment he sees the wallpaper through her is when he understands. (This mirrors his own form blurring when he's afraid.)

**Personality:** Stubborn, opinionated, formal in the way Edwardian children were formal. Slightly archaic speech. Lonely in a way she has stopped admitting. Funny when she lets herself be.

**Her unfinished meal:** Includes brussels sprouts she did not want to finish. After Pip gives her family back to her in clarity, Iris pulls the sprouts up from the floorboards (a *squeeze-and-pop* callback to her own arrival) and Pip and Iris finish the meal *together* — because in the memory her mother told her to finish. Pip's reassurance, *"They're not bad. They're really not."*, lands during the shared meal. The wider Edwardian dinner around them appears as the manifestation cinematic — long table, white tablecloths, many faces, mid-meal — but the specific dishes besides the sprouts remain implementation-level.

**The ability she gives Pip:** **Memory-gifting.** Pip has been collecting memories the whole game (every taste-memory and object-memory adds to his journal). Iris hands Pip her father's switchblade — a small folding blade with a mother-of-pearl handle, given to her so she could sharpen her own pencils — and as Pip touches it, *object-memory* triggers: he sees her family at dinner, alive, mid-meal. The journal records it. He realizes she has been forgetting them slowly, and that he can *give the memory back to her in full clarity*. He focuses, reaches, and sends it. The dining room manifests around her. Her family appears, tells her to finish her meal so she can be with them — *"and eat your sprouts, too"* — and she does. The switchblade stays with Pip; she presses it into his palm before she goes. Empathy is what activated the gift; the ability remains, and grows.

**The lesson Iris teaches Pip:** *Moving on is not forgetting. Moving on is reconnecting.* This is the midpoint pivot of the entire game. Pip will apply this lesson to himself in the final chapter. Iris is his foreshadow.

**After Chapter 5:** Iris has moved on. She does not return as a recurring character (mirroring Erik's resolution — the dead who help us are not stuck with us). But the ability she gave Pip remains, and grows.

## Ghost Physics — visual and writing standard

A standard for how ghosts occupy space in the game's pixel-art visuals and in the writing that describes them. Pixel art renders silhouettes clearly but not contact-points; the writing should not ask the audience to imagine ground-contact a ghost can't actually have.

**What ghosts can do (all ghosts, including Pip and Iris):**

- **Stand and walk** — the default occupancy mode. The audience reads a standing figure as a person in a space without raising questions.
- **Hover and float** — emphasized when the ghost-ness is the point of the moment (Pip rising during Float ability beats; Pip across from another character in conversation; Pip during emotional intensity).
- **Lean** — slight, ambiguous; the lean is felt, the contact is not committed.
- **Bend down to pick something up** — transient, returns to standing. The pose is not held.
- **Pass through objects** — Pip's default; Iris by choice.

**What ghosts cannot do:**

- **Sit, kneel, lie down, or perch.** These are *committed contact poses* that raise the *"why doesn't she fall through the chair?"* question. The pixel art cannot answer it, so the writing shouldn't ask it.

**The exception that proves the rule: *moving on*.** When a ghost crosses the threshold and is no longer a ghost, they can sit. Iris's resolution at the end of Chapter 5 is to take her seat at her family's dinner table — and that sitting is the *visual indicator* that she is gone. The audience reads it correctly because the chapter has set up the threshold-crossing as a transformation. **Sitting at the table is what the living do.** That contrast is load-bearing.

**Pip vs. Iris — practiced vs. new.** Pip is brand new at being a ghost; Iris has been one for over a century. Their physics differ:

- **Pip** floats by default-when-emphasized, passes through objects without thinking, cannot reliably handle solid things, and is *legibly* ghost-like at first glance. His outline blurs when sad or scared and glows warmer when happy.
- **Iris** can pop in and out of frame (her squeeze-and-pop entrance through the floorboards generalizes — she can do this at will), handles solid objects easily (the switchblade, the plate of brussels sprouts, shaking off seawater like a dog), and reads as *solid* by default. She goes translucent only when sad or stubborn. *That's how Pip eventually realizes she's a ghost — when he sees the wallpaper through her.*

**The loss of human particulars, beyond physics.** What ghosts lose isn't only the ability to sit; they lose the small visual particulars of being a person. Pip's hair and his chef's apron — things that made him a boy with a future as a cook — are not part of his ghost form. They appear only in memory. Iris's Edwardian dress is the exception that proves the rule: Iris has been a ghost for over a century, has built an identity around her unfinished dinner, and clings visually to who she was. Pip is *new* to this. He hasn't yet found what to hold onto. He's a clean blank shape. By the end of the game, when Pip moves on, the audience should sense that the boy in the apron is who he gets to be again on the other side.

**Pätu, by contrast, does what cats do.** She sits, kneels (well, crouches), perches on windowsills, lies down, naps. She is alive. The contrast between her freely-physical body and the ghosts' constrained one is part of the chapter rhythm.

**Why this matters for writing.** When drafting scene prose, don't say *"Pip sits across from her"* — say *"Pip is across from her"* or *"Pip floats across from her"*. Don't say *"Iris kneels with the plate"* — say *"Iris holds the plate close, looking at the sprouts"*. The position is implied; the rule is preserved.

## The Grandparents

**Status:** Alive. Living. They are mourning Pip throughout the entire game.

**Names:** Babcia is **Marta**. Dziadek is **Jan**. Pip would have called them Babcia and Dziadek; their first names are spoken by Henrik or by other characters.

**Their function in the story:**
- **Chapter 1:** Seen briefly, grieving, leaving the ship with Pip's body to take him home to Greenpoint.
- **Throughout the game:** Pip occasionally sees them in flashes — through a radio broadcast, in a dream-cinematic, in a recipe that smells like Babcia's kitchen. He cannot reach them. They are the emotional anchor of the story.
- **Final chapter:** When Pip moves on, his last act is to send a taste-memory back to his grandmother in Greenpoint. She is sitting at her kitchen table. She tastes something — pierogi, the way her own mother made them — and weeps, and smiles. She knows. She may whisper *"Filipek?"* — her name for him, the diminutive only she ever used. (TBD at final-chapter planning.)

**This is the gut-punch.** The whole game is building toward that moment. Don't squander it.

## Pätu the Cat

**Role:** A small living anchor in a haunted setting. The ship is full of ghosts and echoes; Pätu is a reminder that life still happens here. She is also a *participant* — she investigates with Pip, senses things, and has an in-game function as a soft hint-giver and as a good judge of character.

**Status:** Alive. Real. Recurring NPC across many chapters. A free cat — she belongs to no one.

**Name:** Estonian. Pronounced *PAH-too*.

**Backstory:** Pätu was born a stray. Leida (the Estonian grandmother in Chapter 2) found her as a freezing kitten, alone, and nursed her back to health. Pätu grew up partly in Leida's cottage, partly outside — she was always free, never owned. She made her way to the harbor, eventually onto *The Mnemosyne*, and the ship became her base. She still visits Leida when the ship ports in Estonia. She still visits other people across other ports. She lives nowhere in particular. She is a cat who chooses.

**The Haldjas's connection:** Leida's family has been protected by the Haldjas — an Estonian house-spirit — for generations. As a favor to Leida, the Haldjas keeps an eye on the stray kitten Leida nursed, even though Pätu won't stay home. The Haldjas is not Pätu's spirit. She is Leida's, doing kindness work outside her remit.

**First appearance:** Chapter 2 (Tallinn). Pip meets her in the lower deck of the ship, with the Haldjas beside her. Pip earns her trust — and the Haldjas's — by helping find Leida's missing cast-iron pan. After the recipe is shared, Pätu chooses Pip. She decides she wants adventure with him and asks to come aboard. Pip promises to watch over her *as long as she chooses to come aboard*. The Haldjas tests his sincerity (the chapter's cleverness puzzle). She is satisfied. From here, Pätu is a recurring shipmate.

**Pip's promise:** *"I'll protect you, Pätu. As long as you choose to come aboard."* This is Pip's first promise in the game. It gets gently tested across the rest of the chapters — Pätu gets into trouble, Pip helps. She is hurt or sad, Pip notices.

**Gameplay function:**
- **Soft hint-giver.** If Pip is taking too long to find an important object, Pätu meows by it. The player who's stuck has a feline assistant.
- **Good judge of character.** Pätu hisses at things that are wrong — bad ghosts, malevolent monsters, situations Pip should be wary of. She doesn't hiss at things that are fine, even when they're frightening (the Karakoncolos got a hiss; the Black Shuck didn't — well, he got generic guard-cat behavior). There is no single canonical bad-ghost beat reserved for her; her judgment surfaces wherever it's earned.
- **Investigator.** She accompanies Pip in some chapters, sniffs out objects, sits near him while he eats with Henrik.
- **Free.** She comes and goes. She is not a tagalong. Some chapters she's there, some she isn't. Her absence is as legible as her presence.

**Function in the larger story:** Pätu is the gentle counter-image to Pip. Pip is a boy tethered to a ship he didn't choose. Pätu is a cat tethered to nothing — she's on the ship because she likes it, not because she has to be. The contrast is quiet but real. When Pip moves on, Pätu will keep doing what she always does: visit people. Stay near Henrik. Be free.

**Visual:** Small. Some Estonian cat-pattern (TBD when art is commissioned). Norwegian-skinny. Yellow eyes.

## The Haldjas

**Role:** An Estonian house-spirit. Appears only in Chapter 2. Named here because she's load-bearing for that chapter's encounter and because *not all ghosts are good* — the foreshadowing she plants — runs through the rest of the game.

**Who she protects:** Leida's family, for generations. The Haldjas is a family spirit — she guards a bloodline and the home it keeps. As a favor to Leida, she also keeps a quiet eye on Pätu, the stray Leida nursed back from cold. The Haldjas does not follow the cat onto the ship. She stays near the cottage. Pätu is, to her, an extension of Leida's care — and Leida's care is what she guards.

**What she does in Chapter 2:** When Pip meets Pätu in the lower deck, the Haldjas is sparkling beside the cat. She challenges Pip's right to be near her — *not all ghosts are good*. The encounter resolves cooperatively: Pip, Pätu, and the Haldjas search together for Leida's missing cast-iron pan. Pip earns trust by helping. After the recipe is shared at Leida's cottage, Pätu asks to come aboard the ship with Pip. Pip promises to watch over her. The Haldjas steps in for the chapter's cleverness puzzle — a sincerity test on the promise. She is satisfied. Leida places a meatball on an existing altar outside the cottage as a thank-you. The Haldjas accepts the offering and stays. Pip and Pätu return to the ship alone.

**After Chapter 2:** The Haldjas does not recur. She is Leida's spirit, not the ship's. Her work was the pan-quest and the sincerity test. The bond she vouched for — Pip and Pätu — continues without her.

**Foreshadowing she plants:** *Not all ghosts are good.* The first monster Pip meets warns him directly. This is the seed for the shadow at the climax — the world has bad ghosts, and Pip will eventually meet one (Pätu's hiss is the first sign). The Haldjas is the bible's first explicit acknowledgement that the spirit-world isn't uniformly kind.

**Visual:** Sparkles in the corner of a room — warm gold-amber, distinct from Pip's cool white and Iris's eventual green-blue. Estonian forest-spirit register.

## The Ghost-World: Neutral Planes

### Neutral planes

The ghost-world doesn't wake the newly-dead in their own beds. That would be too cruel — too jarring to confront one's own body before one knows what's happened. Instead, ghosts wake in *neutral planes*: spaces of habit, transit, or shared use. A hallway. A sidewalk. A kitchen at three in the morning. Spaces that belong to no single person.

Pip wakes in the hallway of the *RMS Mnemosyne*. He has, as far as he can tell, always woken in this hallway. He doesn't remember waking up anywhere else. The ghost-world is being kind to him; it has not yet asked him to remember.

Every chapter's opening uses a neutral plane appropriate to the place. The hallway is Chapter 1's. Each chapter following has its own.

## The Setting: The Mnemosyne (the ship)

A massive world-cruising ocean liner named **The Mnemosyne** *(Nem-OSS-uh-nee)*. Not a specific real ship — fictional, with the bones of a Hurtigruten-style coastal ferry but the scale of a global cruise. It's a constant presence: when Pip isn't ashore, he's aboard.

**Why "Mnemosyne":** Mnemosyne is the Greek goddess of memory and the mother of the Muses. In Greek cosmology she is paired with Lethe — the river of forgetting. Souls drink from Mnemosyne to remember and from Lethe to forget. The two waters are opposites. The ship Pip is aboard is named for memory; the wrecked ship Iris died on (Chapter 5) is named for forgetting. The whole game's premise is encoded in those two names.

**On the pronunciation joke:** The name is a mouthful, and the game knows it. The first time it's introduced, the narrator helps the player gently: *"If you don't speak ancient Greek, Mnemosyne is pronounced Nem-OSS-uh-nee. Yes, it's a mouthful."* Throughout the early chapters the phonetic is included on first use per scene. By later chapters it fades — by Chapter 4 or so, it's just *the ship*. The wink establishes the narrator's voice early: gentle, knowing, food-aware, never cruel.

**Why a ship at all:** It's a moving haunted house. The ship lets the game travel anywhere in the world without losing continuity. It's also a self-contained mystery box — there are decks Pip hasn't explored, locked doors, crew members with their own stories, a haunted past of its own.

**The ship's haunted history.** The Mnemosyne has been afloat long enough to accumulate stories of her own. Past incidents leave residues — small creature-echoes that drift through corridors, particularly at the start of each chapter when Pip is approaching the gangway. The first canonical incident is **the great snail breakout**, an event in the ship's earliest days that left a population of giant candle-lit snail-echoes haunting one stretch of passage. Other incidents will surface as new chapters are designed. The ship is a character with backstory, not just a setting. (See *Echo-creatures* in 02-game-design.md for how these residues function in gameplay.)

**Areas of the ship (to be developed across chapters):**
- **Cabin 646:** Pip's cabin. Where he died. Always accessible. Becomes a "save point" / hub.
- **Hallway / corridors:** Connecting tissue.
- **Grandparents' Cabin:** Adjacent to Pip's. Empty after Chapter 1.
- **The Kitchen:** Henrik's domain. Hub for getting recipe leads.
- **The Dining Room:** Big, ornate, where the living guests eat. Pip can wander here unseen.
- **The Bridge:** The captain. There may be a captain ghost storyline.
- **The Engine Room:** Mechanical, dark, full of pipes. Possible puzzle environment.
- **The Radio Room:** Where Pip first learns to talk through speakers.
- **The Library:** Old maritime books, journals, lore about the regions the ship visits.
- **The Ballroom:** Used at night. Possible dance/music puzzles.
- **Crew Quarters:** Henrik's room is here. Other crew with stories.
- **Henrik's Quarters:** A specific room within crew quarters. Locked at first; unlocks during the photograph-reveal chapter (Ch6 or Ch7, TBD) when Pip earns access. Contains the photograph of Erik and the lefse/gravlaks recipe.
- **The Lower Decks:** Spooky. Storage. Things forgotten.
- **The Lower Deck Storage Room (Chapter 5):** An ordinary lower-deck storage room with old floorboards. When *The Mnemosyne* stalls directly above the wreck of *The Lethe*, Iris squeezes up through the floorboards into this room. There is no flooding — the room is just a room. The chapter's Mamlambo encounter is staged here too: the porthole window is the chapter's interface with the creature in the water below. *(Earlier bible drafts called this "the Drowned Room"; that framing is retired.)*
- **The Snail Hallway:** A passage on a lower deck where the great snail breakout took place. Now permanently inhabited by candle-shelled echo-snails. Pip traverses this in the Brittany chapter (see ports below).
- **The Top Deck:** Open sea, weather, the sense of motion.

### Echo-creatures: one species per chapter

The Mnemosyne carries the residue of every encounter across its history — small spectral animals that drift along baseboards, scuttle across pipes, hang from rafters. The ghost-vermin. They are translucent, ground-level, and slightly drained-looking. Touching one drains a small amount of Pip's strength (canonical echo-creature behavior).

Each chapter features **one species** as its ambient ghost-vermin, deliberately. The species changes by chapter — never repeated, always specific. This gives each port-of-call a distinct ambient register without overwhelming any single environment.

**Chapter 1: echo-mice.** Small, ground-level, drained-amber translucent. They scuttle along baseboards in the dark corridor. They are unattended but not menacing — the chapter's only "small wrongness" beyond the cinematics.

*(Future chapters TBD — the visual research tracker enumerates candidates: echo-spiders, echo-bats, echo-rats, echo-tarsiers, mosquitoes, urban vermin like cockroaches. Each will be assigned to a chapter as that chapter is designed.)*

## The Setting: The Ports

The ship visits ports across the world. Each port is its own chapter (or part of a chapter). Each port has:
- A regional cuisine focus
- A local folkloric character or spirit who serves as a partner-chef
- **A frightening encounter** Pip must get past to reach the chef. The bible's tone (gentle horror, never cruel) still holds — the frightening thing is the *encounter*, not necessarily violence
- A real recipe that gets unlocked on the recipe site when the chapter completes
- Spooky/haunted location aesthetic — even bright cities have haunted corners

**The chapter structure (Ch2 onward).** Each chapter from Chapter 2 onward follows a three-part rhythm: a **wordless traversal** on the ship (echo-creatures, atmosphere, no dialogue), a **monster encounter** at the port (the frightening obstacle, a cleverness puzzle), and a **chef encounter** (the warm reward, a recipe puzzle). The monster is not an enemy — they are an unwilling guide who, once outwitted or appeased, gives up the clue Pip needs to find the chef. Chapter 1 is the exception — Henrik plays both monster-replacement (the moment he sees Pip) and chef-replacement (the first taste) in a single warm-spooky introduction. The rhythm has flex: the monster encounter does not have to be a single tight beat. In Chapter 2 it's a *frame structure* — the Haldjas's wariness opens the chapter, the cooperative pan-quest stretches across the middle, and a closing spell-puzzle resolves it after the chef encounter. The principle is preserved (cleverness, not combat); the staging varies. **Puzzle *form* is themed to the monster.** Each chapter's puzzle is designed in the monster's idiom — drawn from their lore, their domain, the kinds of bargains or tests that creature would naturally pose. The four formats described in `02-game-design.md` (riddle, memory match, ingredient logic, story completion) are starting examples; specific puzzles may sit outside them. Per-chapter puzzle specs live in `chapter-specs/`. (See 02-game-design.md for full mechanics.)

**Inter-chapter porthole transitions.** Between every two consecutive chapters, a brief (~2-3 second) cinematic of the view through a porthole as *The Mnemosyne* travels to the next port. Each transition is unique — a different sea, a different sky, a different time of day. The porthole is the game's metronome: a small held breath between beats. (See 03-art-and-aesthetic.md for the commission list — one porthole per chapter break.)

**The pairs Pip will draw on at the climax:** Every chapter from Chapter 2 onward produces *two* memories — one frightening (a "monster moment") and one feeding (a "meal moment"). Both go into Pip's collected memory inventory. In the final chapter, the shadow plays the dark memory back at Pip; Pip overrides it with the light one. Each chapter's pair is a unit. ## The Seven-Beat Arc

The whole game is structured around classic story shape. Each chapter mirrors this in miniature too.

**1. Exposition.** Pip wakes in Cabin 646 aboard The Mnemosyne. He's dead. He doesn't know it yet. Establish the ship, the loneliness, the warm-but-spooky tone.

**2. Inciting Incident.** Pip discovers he's a ghost (mirror scene). He cannot follow his grandparents off the ship. He meets Henrik, who explains the rules: taste your way through the world, earn your passage. (Chapter 1.)

**3. Rising Action.** Chapters 2 through (n-2). Each port, each meal, each folkloric character. Pip gets better at being a ghost. He collects recipes — *and* the corresponding frightening encounters. He learns that food carries memory. Chapter 4 surfaces Henrietta as a person and plants the photograph; the photograph-reveal chapter (Ch6 or Ch7, TBD) deepens his understanding of why Henrik has been so kind.

**4. Midpoint (Chapter 5: Saldanha Bay).** *The Mnemosyne* stalls above the wreck of *The Lethe*. Iris squeezes through the floorboards of a lower-deck storage room. Pip earns **memory-gifting** in two beats — receiving Iris's family memory through her father's switchblade, then giving it back to her in full clarity — and immediately weaponizes it against the Mamlambo (the chapter's monster, blocking the ship from leaving). Iris's family manifests; she finishes her meal with Pip's help; she moves on. The lesson — *moving on is reconnecting* — pivots Pip's mission. From here, the puzzles get more emotional. He's not just tasting; he's understanding. *(See `ch05-south-africa-outline.md`.)*

**5. Climax (Greenpoint).** Pip returns to his own childhood kitchen. The "monster" is a shadow version of himself — his grief, his fear, his anger for dying — all tangled into one shape that should not exist. Visually: shifting, knotted black wires that won't stay knotted. Inside the static-darkness of the shape, memories play like a TV skipping channels. The recurring channel is the melting face from the Chapter 1 mirror. Pip must confront it.

*Foreshadowing the shadow:* the world has bad ghosts in it, and the bible plants the warning early. The Haldjas tells Pip in Chapter 2 — *not all ghosts are good*. The Karakoncolos in Chapter 4 plants *I don't remember* (the shape of the shadow's voice). The Mamlambo in Chapter 5 introduces the bad-ghost color signature (blood-iron) and shows Pip that warmth is a thing the bad creature cannot hold. Pätu hisses at things that are wrong throughout — she's a steady judge of character, not a single reserved beat. By the time Pip reaches Greenpoint, the player understands that the spirit-world is not uniformly kind, which makes the shadow's existence feel inevitable rather than imposed.

The mechanic: Pip overrides the shadow's painful channels with joyful ones, **in reverse chronological order** — newest first, working backward. Each chapter's "meal moment" answers a "monster moment." The sequence digs down through his life. The final override — the one he reaches last, the one that finishes the shadow — is the pierogi dinner with all six chairs filled: his parents, his grandparents, himself, before any loss had happened. His earliest memory. The one at the bottom of the well.

The shadow does not get defeated through combat. It dissolves through being *answered*. Pip is allowed one beat of anger first — *"It's not fair. I was just a boy."* The shadow gives him permission to say it. And then he answers it anyway.

**6. Falling Action.** Henrik finds Pip in the kitchen, the shadow gone. They make pierogi together. Henrik's hands learn the fold. Pip is teaching his grandmother's recipe to Henrik — so that Henrik will have food to give the next ghost-child who finds him aboard *The Mnemosyne*. The chain continues. The cure for grief is to cook for the next person.

**7. Resolution.** The veil opens. Pip's parents come through. Henrietta and Erik come through. Henrik gets to say goodbye to his family in the same moment Pip says goodbye to his — two grieving people, one kitchen, both finally allowed to release what they have been holding. They usher Pip across.

Pip pauses at the door. He has just had a bite of the pierogi he made with Henrik. His eyes are still half-closed.

> PIP: *Mmmm. That was a perfect last bite.*

He steps through. End.

**Parallel image:** As Pip crosses, in Greenpoint, Babcia is at her kitchen table. She tastes something on the air — pierogi, the way her own mother made them — and weeps, and smiles. She may whisper *"Filipek?"* (TBD.) The game ends on the split image.

## Narrative Voice

The narrator of *The Last Bites* speaks in a specific register: quiet companion, kinetic verbs, gentle parenthetical winks. One verb sharper than expected. Never showy. Food-aware. Self-aware (the Mnemosyne pronunciation joke is a signature).

Reference passage — Chapter 1 opening narration:

> *The hum of engines vibrates with the slow rock of the sea.*
> *Pip opens his eyes with a yawn, still aboard the Mnemosyne.*
> *(If you don't speak ancient Greek, Mnemosyne is pronounced Nem-OSS-uh-nee. Yes, it's a mouthful.)*

When in doubt about how to phrase narration anywhere in the game, return here. *Vibrates with* (verb-based, kinetic — two things touching that shouldn't quite). *Pip opened his eyes with a yawn* (a yawn is alive — the wrongness arrives before he does). *Still aboard* (one word, doing the work of three). The parenthetical (a stage-whisper from a narrator who is on the player's side).

### Voice rules (locked 2026-05-13)

1. **Third person.** Narration always refers to Pip by name or as "he" — never as "you." The narrator describes Pip from outside, like a storyteller watching him move through the ship.

2. **Active voice, present tense.** "Babcia is on the bed," not "Babcia was on the bed." "Pip reaches for the handle," not "The handle is reached for."

3. **Full sentences, not fragments.** Earlier text used phrase-fragments for atmosphere ("Soft. Familiar.") — those are out. Full thoughts in full sentences. The rhythm comes from clean prose, not stylized punctuation.

4. **Spoken dialogue uses quotes and attribution.** PIP's spoken lines: "Babcia, what's wrong?" Pip asks. Or: "Hello!" Pip says, his stomach turning. Quotes around the speech, attribution after, optional narration tail.

5. **Italics = Pip's first-person interior thought.** Interior thoughts are in Pip's own voice — first person, not third. Full sentences, often with attribution trailing. *I should say something. I should say her name.* Or: *How could that be? Pip wonders.* The first-person voice inside the italics is what distinguishes it from narration. The narrator watches Pip; the italics *are* Pip.

6. **Roman = third-person narration.** Description of what's there or what's happening — outside Pip's head, in the narrator's voice. Most lines are roman. Italic is for direct interior voice only. *Babcia is on the bed. Her hands are folded around her red face.*

### The three-register worked example

All three modes in one short passage, using the grandparents' cabin scene:

> Babcia is on the bed. Her hands are folded around her red face.
>
> *She doesn't know I'm here. Pip thinks. She can't hear me.*
>
> "Babciu," Pip says. "Babciu, jestem tutaj."
>
> She does not look up.

- **Roman** = what the narrator observes. Third person, present tense, outside Pip's head.
- **Italics** = what Pip thinks, in his own first-person voice, with attribution trailing.
- **Quoted** = what Pip says aloud, with attribution and optional narration tail.

7. **Sensation has texture.** "Dark water sparkles and dances" rather than "dark water." "A sob breaks through the air" rather than "crying continues." The narration earns its emotional moments through specific physical detail.

### Multilingual dialogue

Characters speak their first language for emotionally-loaded moments. The player understands intent from context — there is no in-game translation UI, no subtitles, no hover-to-translate. The player's inability to translate-perfectly is part of Pip's experience: he is a child encountering languages he doesn't fully speak.

**Examples:**

- Babcia and Dziadek speak Polish to Pip. Babcia's inspection in the grandparents' cabin lands on *"Babciu, jestem tutaj"* ("Babcia, I'm here"). The Polish version is canonical; no English fallback is provided.
- Henrik's mutter is Norwegian. The janitor's mutter is Norwegian.
- Future chapters will use Estonian (Leida, Ch2), Turkish (Muhittin and Omer, Ch4), Indonesian (Ch6 chef), Portuguese (Ch7 chef), Polish again at the climax (Babcia in Ch8).

When a line's emotional weight requires it, the original language is used. When clarity is essential (e.g., the player needs to know what was said for puzzle purposes), the line is in English. The default for atmospheric moments: original language.

## Visual Canon

The game's palette is **a cool world with rare warm pools.** This is bible-level canon and shapes every scene and every character.

- The world (rooms, corridors, ship interiors, ports, the sea, the night sky) is *cool* — deep moonlit blue, soft purple, occasionally cool-green for forested regions. Never gray; always chromatic.
- *Warmth is rare and meaningful.* It appears at the hearth, at the lit window, at the doorway, on the plate of food, in a spirit's signature glow, in Pip's parents at the climax. *Anything that means home, life, or care is warm.* Anything else is cool.
- Pip is *cool-white-translucent* — a small cool light against the cool world. He glows softly because he's a ghost, not because he's warm.
- This palette enacts the central image of the game: a small ghost-boy moves through a cold otherworld looking for the places where someone is still cooking. The warmth he finds is what makes him real for a moment.

The palette also serves accessibility. Blue/amber and purple/amber contrasts work for all common forms of color blindness and resist screen glare. *No player is locked out by color.*

See `03-art-and-aesthetic.md` for the locked tokens, regional palette tokens, and warm-pool placement rules.

## What This Game Is NOT

- Not violent.
- Not jump-scare horror.
- Not a "defeat the boss" game — the chefs are not enemies, they're partners. The monsters are not enemies either; they're unwilling guides.
- Not preachy.
- Not too sad to enjoy. The melancholy must always be balanced by warmth, humor, and the joy of food.
