# Chapter 3 Outline — A Scotsman's Red Curry

The first chapter outside Estonia. Pip's first port-city. Establishes — quietly, before Ch6 will establish it formally — that *the dead can come home*.

This document is the **narrative outline** for the chapter. Implementation-grade detail (full dialogue, cinematic specs) gets written separately when the chapter is being built.

**Working title:** *A Scotsman's Red Curry.* All chapter titles going forward are recipe-themed (the convention applies retroactively — Ch1 and Ch2 titles to be revisited).

**Dedication:** *In memory of Sandy Duncan and his wife Caitlin and children Robert, Archie, Max, Gus, and Bibi.* This text plays as a quick end-credit card at the chapter's very end, fading into the porthole transition to Ch4. Brief, true, not lingering — *life goes on*. (See `06-roadmap-and-open-questions.md` for context on how this chapter came together.)

---

## Position in the arc

Chapter 3 is the second chapter of the rising action — Pip's first port outside Estonia.

- Confirms the chapter rhythm holds outside Estonia (wordless traversal → monster → chef), with the monster encounter as a real puzzle (the Black Shuck) and the chef encounter doubling up with another ghost (Sandy).
- Plants the *visiting-after-moving-on* archetype that Ch6 (Erik's birthday) will formalize. Sandy is Pip's first encounter with a ghost who has *already moved on and chosen to come back*. Pip doesn't know this is what he's seeing. The player feels it.
- Tonally inverts Ch2. Estonia was quiet, single-grandmother, tender. Southampton is *loud, full-house, brash-funny* — but with one moment where Sandy's awareness of his death surfaces.
- Establishes *Pätu chooses* — she's ashore, she leads, she comes and goes on her own terms. Her gameplay function (meowing toward important things, hissing at threats) is shown for the first time in port.

---

## Emotional arc

**What Pip carries in:** Object-memory ability. The journal with two recipes (gravlaks/lefse, kodused kotletid) and the memory of Pätu and the Haldjas. The lesson from Ch2 — *not all ghosts are good*. Pätu is with him today.

**What Pip learns:** Some ghosts have moved on and *come back*. The dead are not gone if someone keeps cooking their food. Family is loud and large and wonderful. *Life's short, mate.*

**What Pip carries out:** A new recipe (Sandy's red curry). A new permission — *visiting is a thing you can do*. A line he will hear again at his climax. A taste-memory of Sandy's kitchen seen through Sandy's own eyes.

The chapter's emotional center is the moment Pip tastes the curry and briefly *becomes* Sandy looking at his family. The chair is occupied. The chair has always been occupied. The chair will keep being occupied as long as Tuesdays come.

## Setting

**The Mnemosyne, lower decks (wordless traversal).** Brief. Crimson carpet, sea-blue light through portholes. The moonlit-blue palette holds. Echo-creatures: small luminous fish drifting through the corridor air — *the ghosts of fish that died for fish-and-chips*. Atlantic cod, haddock, plaice. They glow warm-amber against the cool corridor — the only warmth in the scene.

**Southampton dockyard at night.** Slate-blue and damp. Real Southampton textures — cranes, a wine shop on the pier (Edie's place), a working pub, moored boats, a single streetlamp throwing a warm-amber pool in the rain. The cool-base palette holds; the regional shadow note is teal-green sea-shadow.

**Bevois Street.** A residential street rising from the docks. Narrow roads. Echo-deer cross at intervals. The Dundee house sits midway up — white, big windows pouring amber into the cool blue street. The warmest pool on the street.

**The Dundee kitchen.** Yellow-warm walls. A long pine table that seats nine when needed (Sandy's chair always at the head). A range cooker. Spice jars on a shelf. Sandy's traces visible if you look — a YoYo Games hoodie hanging on a chair, a handwritten recipe card on the counter, a photograph on the fridge present but not zoomed-in, a worn cookbook with marginalia in two handwritings, a child's drawing of a dragon on the fridge.

## Beat 1 — Wordless traversal

Brief. Lower-deck of the Mnemosyne. Pip moves toward the gangway. Pätu pads alongside him — she's coming today.

The luminous fish-echoes drift through the corridor air, schooling near porthole windows. *Atlantic cod, haddock, plaice — the ghosts of fish that died for fish-and-chips.* They glow warm-amber. Pip can pass through them; touching one drains a tiny breath of strength (instant, gentle). Pätu watches them but does not chase.

Pip and Pätu reach the gangway. The North Sea air is cold. The dockyard waits.

**No dialogue. Movement, atmosphere, the sound of the ship arriving.**

---

## Beat 2 — The dock, the man from the sea, and the Black Shuck

Pip and Pätu step onto the dock. Pätu *immediately* runs ahead — toward a man walking out of the sea carrying a bag of groceries. Pip stops and stares.

The man is ghost-translucent. Pip has never seen another ghost-figure walk out of water like he was always there. The man doesn't notice them; he's heading up the dock with purpose. Pätu trots after him. Pip follows from behind.

Some back-and-forth between Pip and Pätu as they go — *placeholder dialogue beat to be drafted, but the texture is: Pip catching up to Pätu, asking her quietly what she's doing, Pätu answering only in her cat-way (a meow, a glance back, a continuing trot). She knows. He doesn't yet.*

**The Black Shuck appears.** A massive black dog blocks the road ahead — a real folkloric figure from English coastal lore, an omen of death, eyes glowing. He stands between Pip-and-Pätu and the path the man-from-the-sea is taking.

In folklore, Black Shuck is not evil — he's an omen, a marker that someone has died. He stands at thresholds. The chapter holds him at this register: he's not malevolent, but he's *in the way*, and he's hungry.

### The puzzle — feeding the Black Shuck

A real cleverness puzzle, keyed to the Shuck's idiom. *You feed an omen-dog to pass him.*

**Visual layout:** Conveyor-belt mini-game. A line of dock-trash moves past Pip — pieces of meat (the right answer) interspersed with seagulls, seaweed, and rubbish (obstacles). Pip selects the meat at the right time, aims (a dotted line shows where the Shuck stands), and throws.

**Mechanics:**
- **Up arrow** — select meat as it passes / shoot the meat once aimed.
- **Space bar** — float, to avoid obstacles (seagulls swooping, seaweed flapping, trash flying).
- **Goal:** three successful feedings. Each successful throw — the Shuck catches the meat in his mouth and chews.
- **Failure state:** if Pip is hit by an obstacle, the meat is knocked from his hands. New meat comes by. **No life lost, no progress lost** — just try again. (Per `02-game-design.md`'s no-fail rule.)
- **Pätu's role:** sits beside Pip, watches the dog, hisses at the Shuck periodically. She's a tonal presence, not a hint-giver. *(Note: the Shuck is a folkloric omen, not a haunting bad ghost — Pätu's hiss here reads as guard-cat behavior rather than a deeper alarm.)*

**Resolution:** After three successful feedings, the Shuck barks once — a deep, satisfied bark — and trots off down the dock, content. Pip and Pätu can pass. The road ahead is clear.

The puzzle takes maybe 60-90 seconds and is designed to feel kinetic and gentle simultaneously — *you fed the omen, and the omen went home.*

## Beat 3 — Edie at the wine shop

Pip and Pätu run into Edie at her wine shop on the pier. *(Note: in earlier drafts she ran a chip-shop. The wine shop is the working version — it serves the chapter's wine-bottle errand.)* She's an old Cockney ghost, decades on these docks, ghost-translucent behind her counter. She's been watching.

> EDIE: *That man there from the sea comes through on Tuesdays. Cooks for his family. They still miss him like it was the day after he passed. They never let on, but Caitlin always makes too much rice. Off you go. And tell that bloody Scotsman he forgot to pick up the wine again.*

She hands Pip a bottle of white wine. Pip takes it carefully — it's a real object now in his inventory. The wine bottle is the chapter's running thread; it travels with him from dock to kitchen.

Edie's register is brash, English-coastal, kind. She and Sandy are a matched pair — *she's the dock-version of being-placed-and-useful, he's the family-version*. They never meet on screen.

---

## Beat 4 — The walk up Bevois Street

Pip and Pätu traverse the narrow streets up to the Dundee house. Echo-deer cross between buildings at intervals — translucent, warm-amber, gone in a moment. They're not threats; they're texture. Southampton at night, full of small ghosts moving through.

The Dundee house comes into view — white walls, big windows, amber light pouring into the cool blue street. Pip can hear voices through the door from a block away. A girl laughing. A boy shouting about a fish. A brash Scottish voice mid-laugh.

Pip walks through the front wall. Pätu trots around the side of the house — *to be seen again later, in her own time*.

## Beat 5 — The kitchen, arrival

The household, when Pip arrives:

- **Caitlin** at the range, stirring curry. Short blond pixie cut. The kitchen's center of gravity.
- **Robert** at the kitchen table, reading a book. Tall, brown hair, muscular.
- **Archie** at the counter, cutting up a fish. Tall, skinny, brown hair.
- **Max and Gus** near the doorway, talking. Slightly shorter than Archie, brown hair.
- **Bibi** at the counter, making boba tea. The youngest, about Pip's age, long light brown hair.
- **Sandy** at Caitlin's left shoulder. Ghost-translucent like Pip but brighter, fuller, more present. He turns when Pip walks in.

> SANDY: *Oh hello, mate. You're new.*

Pip stares for a beat. Then offers the bottle.

> PIP: *Hi, sir. The lady at the wine store said you forgot this and sent me to give it to you.*

Sandy grins — a big, brash, Scottish grin.

> SANDY: *Well, that'll do.*

He uncorks the bottle, pours himself a glass, and leaves the rest on the counter for Caitlin and the boys (who are adults). Caitlin reaches for the limes without looking; Sandy nudges them closer; her hand finds them. *It's just where the limes are.*

**The family does not see Sandy directly.** But they're not unaware. They've been moving around him for a year, or five years, or however long Sandy has been gone.

## Beat 6 — Sandy teaches Pip the curry

The chapter's recipe-acquisition. Format C from `02-game-design.md` (ingredient logic), taught conversationally — not a test, an absorption.

**The setup:** Caitlin cooks. Sandy directs from her shoulder. Pip's job is to select ingredients from the counter and push them toward Caitlin as she needs them, at Sandy's instruction. Three-way collaboration. Caitlin is the hands, Sandy is the voice, Pip is the apprentice.

**The recipe — Sandy's red curry — built across the scene:**

1. **Red curry paste** — coats the vegetables and tofu first. The base.
2. **Onions, ginger, garlic, chilis, bell pepper** — add to the paste-coated tofu and vegetables. Sandy on the chili: *"You go too soft on the heat, you might as well serve them porridge."*
3. **Stir until hot** — the paste activates, the kitchen smells.
4. **Coconut milk** — poured in last. The simmer begins.

The puzzle lives in Pip selecting the right ingredient at the right time and pushing it across the counter. Wrong choices get Sandy's warm correction — *"No no no, mate, that's tarragon, you absolute disaster."* No fail state. By the time the curry is simmering, Pip knows how to make it.

### The cooking dialogue — Sandy's voice through the technique

Between cooking instructions, Sandy talks. The cameos are *interleaved* with technique — not standalone joke beats, but Sandy doing what Sandy did at every stove he ever stood at: telling stories while he worked.

**Three named beats during cooking:**

**The Mike beat (mid-vegetable).** Sandy on not overcooking the vegetables:

> SANDY: *Trick is, don't overcook the vegetables. My mate Mike never learned that. Cooks his steak till it's leather. Like he's punishing it. I tell him — you don't grill a cow, you have a conversation with it. He doesn't listen. Loves him anyway. Just don't ever let him cook your dinner.*

Caitlin smiles at no one in particular. Bibi pipes up: *"Mike's silly."* Sandy: *"Bibi knows. Bibi gets it."*

**The Jesse beat (mid-coconut-milk pour).** Sandy drifts:

> SANDY: *This American lad Jesse I know loves this curry. He's been coming up to visit more before...*

Sandy trails off. Just for a beat. Then he picks up the spoon and stirs the pan.

> SANDY: *Hard man, Jesse. Climbed a mountain with me once after we'd been drinking till two in the morning. Good lad.*

The trailing-off is the chapter's one moment where Sandy's awareness surfaces — he knows time has passed, he knows what *before* meant. He moves past it the way he always moved past hard things: with a story. Caitlin glances toward Sandy's chair; her expression softens. *The story has been told before. She knows it. She loves it.*

**The Michel beat (just before dinner is ready).** Sandy looks at his watch:

> SANDY: *Michel should be arriving soon with dessert.*

The doorbell rings. Caitlin wipes her hands. Robert looks up from his book.

A French man — **Michel** — and his **brunette wife** walk in. Greetings. Coats off. Dessert handed over. *Brief, warm, real.* They take seats at the table, which now has nine settings instead of seven.

The Michel beat lands quietly. Michel and his wife are present, but they're not the chapter's focus — they're *with* the family. Like they always have been. Like they always will be.

## Beat 7 — Archie almost-sees Sandy

A small recurring beat across the kitchen scene. Three small moments:

**First:** mid-cooking, Archie at the counter cutting his fish, Sandy says something funny, and Archie pauses mid-cut. He looks toward the room — toward Sandy's chair, almost. *"Did you hear something?"* he says, to no one in particular. The room glances toward Sandy's place. *"Almost sounded like..."* He trails off. Goes back to the fish.

**Second:** Sandy passes near Archie at the counter and Archie shivers a tiny shiver, then smiles a tiny smile. Caitlin sees it from across the room. Her expression softens. She doesn't say anything.

**Third:** at the dinner table, Archie's eyes drift to the head of the table and stay there for a beat too long. Caitlin's hand finds his shoulder. Still no one says anything. The player feels what's happening without it being named.

## Beat 8 — The dinner

Caitlin ladles curry into bowls. The family sits down at the table — nine seats, all occupied:

- Caitlin at one end.
- Robert next to her.
- Archie next.
- Max and Gus side by side.
- Bibi.
- Michel.
- Michel's wife.
- And **Sandy at the head of the table** — ghost-translucent, holding a translucent bowl. He doesn't eat. He just sits there with them.

The family eats. The kids talk. Robert continues some thread about a fishing trip. Bibi narrates her own meal. Michel and his wife are warmly part of the table — quiet, but there.

### Pip tastes the curry — the chapter's deepest beat

Pip stands at the doorway with a small bowl Caitlin set aside for him without quite knowing why she did. He takes a bite.

**The taste-memory triggers.** For a held second, Pip sees the kitchen *through Sandy's eyes* — a similar dinner from before, a year ago, five years ago, whenever — Sandy at the stove cooking, the family already gathered, the chairs filling. The image of *being the one who feeds them*. The warmth of doing it. The completeness.

The memory holds for a beat. Then Pip is back at the doorway, watching the family eat the curry he just helped make.

**The chapter's lesson lands here.** Not in dialogue, not in a clever exchange. *In a tasted memory of what it felt like to be Sandy, holding a family at a table.* Pip will reach for this image at the climax — *I can do this too. I can be the one who comes back.*

---

## Beat 9 — Sandy's parting line

As the kids clear their plates and start washing up, Pip says thank you to Sandy. Sandy walks Pip to the door. They pause at the threshold. Sandy is brash even now, but a little quieter — the way he was at the end of nights.

> SANDY: *Right then. On you go, ghost-boy. And listen — you're not actually gone if you just keep showing up. Doesn't matter how. Just show up. Life's short, mate.*

Pip walks out the door. Pätu is waiting on the front step, meowing — she's been there for a while, knew when to come back. The two of them walk down Bevois Street toward the docks. The Dundee house glows warm behind them.

**On the walk back to the ship:** Pätu pauses at a small newsstand near the harbor — closed for the night, shutters down — and meows, sharp and intentional, at a small box of matches sitting on a wooden crate by the door. *(Pattern callback: Pätu's meow-at-objects. She did it in Ch2 with the Haldjas's altar; she does it now.)* Pip pockets the matches. *(He doesn't know yet what they're for. The candle from Henrik has been in his pocket since Ch1; matches finish the pair. Both are load-bearing for Ch4's Karakoncolos puzzle.)*

They walk the rest of the way to the gangway. Pätu trots ahead. Pip follows.

As Pip walks down the cool blue street, the warm house fading behind him, a quiet text card fades in over the image:

> *In memory of Sandy Duncan and his wife Caitlin and children Robert, Archie, Max, Gus, and Bibi.*

Holds for 4-5 seconds. Then fades into the porthole transition to Ch4. *Life goes on.*

## Memory pair for the climax

- **Monster moment:** the Black Shuck — *the omen who could be fed and sent home*. At the climax, Pip will face the shadow (a much darker omen). The lesson from this chapter is that omens can sometimes be answered with kindness rather than confrontation. Pip may try this on the shadow. It may not work. The shadow may not be hungry; the shadow may want something else. But Pip having tried first matters.
- **Meal moment:** the taste-memory through Sandy's eyes. *Being the one who feeds them.* This is one of Pip's two deepest memories he carries to the climax — it teaches him, in the climactic act, that *he can be Sandy too*. He can come back. He can keep showing up. His chair can stay occupied at Babcia's table.

## Abilities used

- **Object-memory** — used optionally on Sandy's recipe card on the counter (one-second flash, no narration override; the player catches it if they want to).
- **Taste-memory** — used in Beat 8, when Pip eats the curry and briefly sees through Sandy's eyes. This is the chapter's most powerful use of the ability so far. Worth noting: *taste-memory triggered by another ghost's cooking* is a new wrinkle — Pip isn't tasting Sandy's grief, he's tasting Sandy's *joy of feeding*.
- **Walk-through-walls** — Beat 4, entering the Dundee house.
- **Float** — Beat 2, dodging obstacles in the Black Shuck puzzle.

## What this chapter is NOT

- **Not a grief chapter, but not grief-free.** The chapter's main register is *love that continues* — Sandy alive in his kitchen, the family alive, brashness and curry and noise. But the chapter has *one* moment where Sandy's awareness of his death surfaces (the Jesse beat, the trailing-off). This is intentional. The trailing-off tells the player *Sandy knows*, which makes everything else (the family not seeing him, the chair always occupied) read as choice rather than obliviousness. The grief catches him for a beat. Then he moves past it the way he always moved past hard things — with a story. *The chapter is honest about what it is, but it doesn't dwell.*
- **Not a tribute that requires recognizing the tribute.** A player who never knew Sandy will play this chapter and feel they've met a brash funny Scotsman who comes home Tuesdays to cook for his family. The chapter works at that level. The deeper layer — the cameos, Jesse-on-the-mountain, Michel-and-his-wife, the dedication card at the end — is held by those who knew. The chapter doesn't break for them, and it doesn't break for those who didn't.
- **Not Pip's chapter to feel sad in.** Pip is *learning* in this chapter, not grieving. The taste-memory through Sandy's eyes is the chapter's deepest beat for him — it's a moment of *understanding*, not sorrow. The grief he carries from his own life isn't surfaced here.
- **Not the chapter where Sandy tells Pip what happened to him.** Sandy doesn't explain his own death. The word *cancer* never appears. He's not in the chapter to be a death story; he's in the chapter to be a *visit*. The Jesse-trailing-off is the closest the chapter gets, and it stops there. The player who knows, knows. The player who doesn't, doesn't need to.
- **Not where *play more* appears.** The original Sandy line *"Life's short. Play more."* is trademarked from the Xbox commercial. The chapter uses *"Life's short, mate"* as a respectful nod — same emotional weight, same character, no trademark conflict. Do not restore the original phrasing.
