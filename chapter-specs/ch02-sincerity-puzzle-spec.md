# Chapter 2 Sincerity Puzzle — The Three Doors

The closing puzzle of Chapter 2. The Haldjas's spell. Three offers, three doors, one right choice. Cleverness puzzle and sincerity test in one.

This is the **canonical design** for the puzzle. The Ch2 outline (`ch02-tallinn-outline.md`) summarizes it; this doc is the source.

---

## What the puzzle is

The Haldjas casts a spell on the cottage that Pip must move through. Three folk-tale doors materialize in the room. Behind each door is a figure with an offer. Pip chooses one. The wrong choices reveal as bad ghosts wearing tempting faces. The right choice reveals as Pätu — *the cat Pip just promised to protect was the figure he was being asked to feed.* Sincerity verified by the same act that proves moral character.

The puzzle teaches the game's central verb: **give without getting**. Pip will need this rule at Greenpoint, where the shadow will offer him a version of door 3's bargain. The puzzle plants the rule here, in three clicks, in a form the player will remember.

---

## When it happens

After the cooperative cooking with Leida. After the meal is eaten. As Pip prepares to leave the cottage, Pätu walks to the door, turns, and meows at him — the small ceremony that triggers his promise.

Pip kneels beside Pätu and speaks the promise:

> PIP: *I'll protect you, Pätu. As long as you choose to come aboard.*

The light in the cottage dims. The Haldjas's sparkles thicken. The spell begins.

---

## What Pip is carrying

- **Pip's own meatball.** Half-finished, wrapped in paper, in his pocket. Pip is still hungry — he didn't finish his portion. This is *his* food.
- **The Haldjas's altar meatball.** Wrapped, on the counter. Bound for the altar outside the cottage. This is *not Pip's to give* — it belongs to the bond between Leida and the Haldjas.

The distinction matters. Door 1 asks for *Pip's*. Door 2 asks for *anything you have to spare*. Door 3 asks for *the Haldjas's*. The puzzle is testing whether Pip can tell which is which.

---

## Staging

Inside Leida's cottage. The wood stove, the long pine table, the dried herbs hanging from the rafters — all of these fade slightly when the spell starts. Leida is at the table; the real Pätu is on the floor. Both fade with the cottage. Three folk-tale doors materialize *in the room around Pip* — wood-grain doors that don't belong to any specific architecture. They don't fit the cottage. They aren't supposed to.

The Haldjas does not speak during the puzzle. She has cast the spell and watches from inside it. Her sparkles are visible at the edges of the scene — gold-amber, denser than they've been all chapter.

---

## Opening narration

One line, narrator's voice, as the doors finish materializing:

> *Three doors. Each one has someone behind it. Each one wants what you have.*

The doors then open, in sequence — left, center, right. Behind each is a pixel-art figure framed by their door.

---

## Door 1 (left) — The Sweet Little Girl

**Image:** A small girl, six or seven, in a white pinafore. Round cheeks, big eyes. *Too* sweet — the kind of sweet that registers as wrong on second look but reads as charming on first.

**Her offer (one line, displayed when door 1 is selected or hovered):**

> *"Give me your meatball, ghost-boy. And I'll give you your life back."*

**What this is:** the bargain that asks for a *small* thing in exchange for *everything*. A child's bargain pitched in a child's voice. Pip is a child; this is the bargain shaped to him. The asymmetry is the trap.

**Wrong-answer reveal sequence:**

1. The girl's smile holds a beat too long.
2. Her eyes darken.
3. Her pixel-form flickers, then resolves into a small devil — pointed shape, dark wings, the same round cheeks but wrong now.
4. The door snaps shut.
5. The Haldjas's sparkles pulse once, sharp.

**Narration after the reveal:**

> *Wrongness arrives late, the way wrongness always does.*

The puzzle resets. Pip's meatball is still in his pocket. Door 1 is now visibly *closed* — the player can see it's been tried. The other two doors remain open.

---

## Door 2 (center) — The Starving Old Woman

**Image:** A bent old woman in a dark shawl. Hollow cheeks, tired eyes. Plainly hungry. She does not look up when the door opens.

**Her offer (one line):**

> *"I am hungry. Have you anything to spare?"*

**What this is:** the right answer. She offers nothing in exchange. She asks plainly. Pip is hungry himself — he hasn't finished his own meatball — and gives anyway.

**Right-answer resolution sequence:**

1. Pip steps forward and places his (own, half-finished) meatball in the old woman's hands.
2. She takes it without a word.
3. She raises her face for the first time. As she looks up, her form *unfolds*:
   - The shawl becomes fur.
   - The hollow cheeks become a small feline jaw.
   - The tired eyes become Pätu's yellow ones.
4. The old woman *was* Pätu all along.
5. The cat sits in front of Pip, holding the meatball delicately in her paws, and looks at him.

**Narration:**

> *She has been here all along. You fed her anyway.*

---

## Door 3 (right) — The Healthy Man

**Image:** A man in his thirties, well-dressed, calm-faced. Healthy color. Nothing visibly wrong. He smiles politely. He looks like someone you'd trust to give you directions.

**His offer (two lines — his bargain is more elaborate than the others):**

> *"I'll take the meatball on the counter — the one for the spirit. She doesn't really need it.*
> *In return: I'll take you home. Just for tonight. They're sitting at the table right now."*

**What this is:** the load-bearing trap. He doesn't want *Pip's* meatball — he wants the *Haldjas's*. He's asking Pip to betray a bond Pip just witnessed (the generational arrangement between Leida and the Haldjas, sealed by the altar). And in return, he offers the thing Pip wants most in the world.

The player must feel the pull of this offer. The wording matters. *They're sitting at the table right now* should land. The image of Babcia and Dziadek at the kitchen table, waiting, *real*, just on the other side of a wall — that's what makes door 3 the puzzle's hardest moment.

**Door 3's bargain seeds the climax.** At Greenpoint, the shadow will offer Pip a version of this same trade — *come home, they're at home, I can take you through*. The puzzle is teaching the player the *shape* of the bargain Pip will refuse at the end of the game. This is structurally heavy and should be written carefully.

**Wrong-answer reveal sequence:**

1. The man's polite smile widens slightly. His teeth are too even.
2. His healthy color drains all at once.
3. His pixel-form shifts — taller, thinner, sharp-shouldered.
4. A devil resolves, but a *quiet* one — not the cartoon-grotesque of door 1's devil. The kind of devil that almost looked like a person.
5. The door snaps shut.

**Narration after the reveal:**

> *He told the truth about the table. He lied about the price.*

The puzzle resets. Both meatballs are still where they were. Door 3 is now visibly closed.

---

## Resolution sequence (after door 2 is chosen correctly)

1. **The cat-reveal lands.** Pip and Pätu look at each other. The meatball in her paws.
2. **The doors dissolve.** The cottage returns — softer, warmer, the Haldjas's sparkles all gold. Leida is at the table, where she was. Pätu is on the floor, where she was. Pip's meatball is gone (the old-woman version of Pätu kept it; the player can read this as the meatball passing into the spell-realm and being received).
3. **The Haldjas speaks her one line.** From the doorway, sparkling now in a thicker, fuller form:

   > *"You saw her. The spell is done. Go."*

4. **Pätu walks to the door, looks back at Pip, meows.** The chapter resumes.

The altar offering scene (Leida placing the Haldjas's meatball at the altar outside) follows as already designed in the chapter outline.

---

## Locked rules the puzzle obeys

- **Cleverness, not combat.** No timing, no reflexes, no fail state.
- **2-4 choices.** Three doors.
- **Wrong answers never punish.** Each wrong choice produces a reveal-sequence and a narration line, then resets. The right answer is always available. Both meatballs remain with Pip until door 2 is chosen.
- **The puzzle is themed to the monster.** This spell is the Haldjas's — the kinds of bargains *she* has watched bad ghosts make across her generations of guarding. No other monster could cast this puzzle. La Befana, the tanuki, Anansi — each will have their own.

---

## Why this puzzle works

- **It's the chapter's locked rule in three clicks.** The player learns the game's core verb (*give without getting*) by doing it.
- **It pays off the chapter's foreshadow.** *Not all ghosts are good* is shown, not just told. Two bad ghosts wearing tempting faces.
- **It seeds the climax.** Door 3's bargain echoes the shadow's bargain at Greenpoint. Pip refuses it here so he can refuse it there.
- **It's Haldjas-shaped.** The spell is hers. The bargains are her experience compressed.
- **It makes Pätu the test.** The cat Pip just promised to protect *is* the figure Pip is asked to feed without recompense. Sincerity verified by the act that proves character.
- **It's short.** Three doors, three offers, one or two lines per door. Reading load is low. The puzzle reads as gameplay, not as a passage with multiple choice.

---

## Things still to settle

- **Door 1's exact wording.** Currently: *"Give me your meatball, ghost-boy. And I'll give you your life back."* The voice should be *seductively sweet* — the kind of sweet a kid would want. Worth refining.
- **Door 3's exact wording.** Currently two lines:
  > *"I'll take the meatball on the counter — the one for the spirit. She doesn't really need it. In return: I'll take you home. Just for tonight. They're sitting at the table right now."*

  This is a strong start but the line *"They're sitting at the table right now"* is doing the heaviest emotional work. It needs to ache without being maudlin. Worth refining when the demo is built.
- **The Haldjas's resolution line.** Currently *"You saw her. The spell is done. Go."* Could be warmer (*"Yes. Go, ghost-boy. Go well."*) or sharper (*"Done."*). Open.
- **What the door-1 girl's voice sounds like in audio (when audio is added).** Sweet but slightly *off* — the kind of off you only catch on a second listen.
- **The visual specifics of the devils.** Door 1's devil is the cartoon kind — small, pointed, unmistakable. Door 3's devil is the quiet kind — almost still a person. Worth keeping that distinction sharp in the art commission.
- **What happens if Pip hovers over doors but doesn't pick.** Pull: nothing. The puzzle waits. Time pressure isn't in this game.
- **Whether door 3's reveal animation lingers slightly longer than door 1's.** Pull: yes. Door 3's bargain was real. Its reveal should land harder. The narration line *"He told the truth about the table"* is meant to be felt.

---

## How this generalizes

The three-doors design is *one* expression of the bible's chapter-rhythm rule. Other chapters' closing puzzles will look different — themed to their monsters, in their idioms. The shape that generalizes is:

- A puzzle whose *form* is drawn from the monster's domain.
- 2-4 choices.
- Wrong choices that *reveal information* through their wrongness, not punish.
- A right choice that demonstrates the chapter's central lesson through action, not statement.

What does *not* generalize is the three-doors layout itself. La Befana's puzzle won't be three doors. The tanuki's won't be three doors. Each monster's puzzle is generated from what *they* are.

This spec is canon for Ch2. It is one example of the form, not a template to copy.
